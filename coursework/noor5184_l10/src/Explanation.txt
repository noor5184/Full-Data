Based on the results from test_Sorts_array, the best and worst really depend on how the data starts out. On already sorted data the winners are the simple algorithms like Insertion Sort, Binary
 Insertion Sort, Bubble Sort, and Cocktail Sort as they do almost no swaps and only about n-1 comparisons, so they’re extremely efficient when the list is ordered. On reverse ordered data,
  those same simple methods become very bas as Bubble and Insertion explode to roughly O(n²) comparisons and swaps, while algorithms like Merge Sort, Heap Sort, and Quick Sort stay close to
   O(n log n) and look much better. On random data, the consistently strongest algorithms are the O(n log n) ones as Merge Sort, Heap Sort, Quick Sort, and often Shell Sort / Comb Sort, while Bubble 
   Sort and Cocktail Sort tend to be the worst overall, with Selection Sort doing a fixed large number of comparisons regardless of input order. So there isn’t a single absolute best algorithm in 
   every situation, but in terms of overall efficiency across random and worst-case inputs, the divide is pretty clear as the O(n log n) sorts (Merge/Heap/Quick) are the best general choices, 
   and Bubble/Cocktail are the worst overall except in the very special case where the data is already nearly sorted.
